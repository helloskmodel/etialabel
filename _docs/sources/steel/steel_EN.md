## 2. Why the Steel Industry Needs Heat-Resistant Labels

What makes steel production different is this: **at the moment material needs to be identified, it is often still red-hot, covered in scale, and about to enter an even harsher process.**

Conventional labels all fail here:

- **Paper / PET labels** — carbonize above 200℃
- **Inkjet coding / paint** — sits on the scale layer; once the scale flakes off, the mark goes with it, and it burns away completely in the reheating furnace
- **Manual tagging** — requires personnel to approach a 700℃ heat source; slow and unsafe
- **Stamping / punch marking** — not machine-readable, cannot connect to MES in real time

The value of a heat-resistant label is keeping **the barcode machine-readable throughout the entire hot-working chain**, genuinely binding physical material to the information system.

## 3. Types of Label Application in the Steel Industry

### Type 1: Direct Hot Application Labels

- **Where:** Slabs and billets straight out of continuous casting; coils after hot rolling
- **Core challenge:** Surface temperature at the moment of application is 550–750℃; the surface is rough and covered in scale. The label must bond instantly and must not burn.
- **How it works:** A robotic labeling system applies the label automatically at the roller table — personnel never touch the heat source.
- **What it solves:** Hot material has a unique identity from the moment it is produced — no more "remember the position" or "estimate the heat number."

### Type 2: Through-Process Labels for Heat Treatment

- **Where:** Pickling and annealing of wire and bar; hot forging and long-cycle annealing of forged parts
- **Core challenge:** The label doesn't just need to survive a brief heat spike — it must **first be immersed in acid (H₂SO₄ / HCl), then be baked in a furnace for 10–18 hours.** Chemical attack and long-duration heat stack on top of each other.
- **How it works:** Applied at ambient temperature; the label travels with the workpiece through the entire heat-treatment chain and is still scannable at furnace exit.
- **What it solves:** Heat treatment is where batches are most often lost. If the label survives, traceability is unbroken.

### Type 3: Heat Treatment Tags

- **Where:** Reusable jigs, fixtures, baskets, and molds that cycle repeatedly
- **Core challenge:** Not single-use — must stay readable through **dozens to hundreds of thermal cycles**
- **How it works:** Mechanically fixed (wired / riveted) to the asset for the long term
- **What it solves:** Tooling asset management, cycle counting, end-of-life alerts

### Type 4: On-Site Printing & System Integration

- **Where:** Runs through all of the above
- **What it is:** Thermal transfer printer + high-temperature ribbon. MES pushes the heat number / batch number in real time, the label is printed and applied immediately, and downstream scanning stations feed data back.

## 2. The Specific Application

**The scene:** Billets cut from the continuous caster, with end-face / side-face temperatures around 750℃, covered in scale that keeps flaking off. Billets wait on the cooling bed, get transported, get stacked, and eventually go into the reheating furnace or straight to the mill.

**The pain at this step:**
Between the caster cut and stacking, the billet has no physical identity. The common approach today is "record the position + log the heat number by hand" — and the moment the stack order gets disturbed or a job is inserted, batch attribution is lost. Inkjet coding cannot form a legible mark on 750℃ scale. Manual tagging requires an operator to carry a tagging pole up to a hot billet.

**How HP-700T works here:**
A robotic labeling station is installed downstream of the cutting machine. MES pushes the heat number → thermal transfer printer prints on the spot → the robot picks the label → it is applied automatically to the billet end face. On contact, the adhesive layer softens and penetrates the rough pores of the scale, forming a mechanical anchor as it cools. The barcode stays scannable while the billet cools on the bed, is moved by crane, and sits in the stack; before furnace entry, a scanning station confirms identity and feeds it back to MES.

**Recommended application point:** The billet end face (thinner, flatter scale), avoiding the cut burr zone.
