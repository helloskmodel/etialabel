## Sector intro — "Circuit Board & PCB Labels"
PCB labels — also called printed circuit board markers — provide track-and-trace
identification through assembly, testing, repair and maintenance. PCB manufacturers use
multi-pass, high-temperature reflow and wave-soldering to join the many connections on a
board — processes involving pre-heat and thermal soaks, highly active (ORH1) fluxes and
high-pressure chemical washes. Polyonics® polyimide PCB label materials withstand the
severity of these processes, maintaining printed barcodes and images through the harshest,
multi-cycle manufacturing. The result: accurate inventory control and traceability from
production through to customer delivery. All materials are REACH & RoHS compliant.

## Sub-categories — Process / Challenge / Recommended Material

### Wash and Reflow
- Process: The label is applied before soldering and remains on the PCB through reflow or wave soldering, flux exposure, chemical cleaning, and high-pressure washing.
- Challenge: Extreme heat, active fluxes, and aggressive cleaners can cause ordinary labels to shrink, lift, discolor, or lose barcode readability.
- Recommended Material: High-temperature polyimide materials with heat-resistant coatings and aggressive adhesives. Standard and ESD-safe constructions are available.

### Wash and Non-Reflow
- Process: The label is applied after reflow but before chemical cleaning or high-pressure washing. It encounters aggressive cleaners and moderate heat without passing through the full reflow profile.
- Challenge: Cleaning chemicals and wash pressure can damage printed images, soften the label coating, or cause edge lifting.
- Recommended Material: Chemical-resistant polyimide or polyester materials selected according to the cleaner, wash pressure, and temperature exposure. ESD-safe options are available.

### Post-Process Identification
- Process: The label is applied after soldering, reflow, and cleaning have been completed. It carries final serial numbers, barcodes, inspection data, or warranty information.
- Challenge: Limited space, uneven surfaces, repeated handling, and long service life require reliable adhesion and clearly readable small-format identification.
- Recommended Material: Durable polyester materials provide a practical and cost-effective solution. ESD-safe polyester options are available for sensitive assemblies.

### Laser-Markable Labels
- Process: Serial numbers, text, barcodes, and 2D codes are created directly on a laser-sensitive label surface. Depending on the construction, the label can remain on the PCB through wash and reflow processes.
- Challenge: The marked image must maintain high contrast and precise code definition despite heat, chemicals, cleaning, and abrasion.
- Recommended Material: Laser-markable polyimide materials for permanent, high-resolution PCB identification. Standard and ESD-safe constructions are available.

## ESD-Safe Options (shared explainer)
Where needed: ESD-safe constructions may be required across all four PCB labeling processes
(wash and reflow, wash and non-reflow, post-process identification, laser marking),
particularly when labels are applied, removed, or repositioned near static-sensitive
components or within an ESD-controlled production area.
Challenge: Conventional label films, adhesives, and liners can generate electrostatic charge
when a label is peeled from its liner or removed from a PCB — a risk for sensitive components
even when the label is heat- and chemical-resistant.
Value: ESD-safe constructions combine static-dissipative surfaces with low-charging adhesives
and release liners to reduce charging during liner removal and label removal/repositioning,
protect static-sensitive components, support controlled electronics-manufacturing workflows,
and maintain traceability without introducing unnecessary ESD risk.
