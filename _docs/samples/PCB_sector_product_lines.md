### Identification Before the Harshest Processes

Labels applied before soldering and cleaning must remain attached and readable after the process—not merely survive the product’s final service environment. Reflow and wave-solder applications can combine rapid temperature change, flux, direct or indirect solder exposure, solvents and aqueous cleaning.

Polyimide is commonly selected because it combines heat resistance with a thin, dimensionally stable printable face. Performance still depends on the complete construction: face material, adhesive, print topcoat, ribbon, PCB surface and process profile.

### Choose by Requirement

- **Low-profile white identification:** XF-533
- **Amber material for demanding high-heat processing:** XF-542
- **Ultra-thin ESD control:** XF-101SE, XF-101ME or XF-101M
- **Standard-profile ESD control:** XF-102SE
- **Harsh-wash print performance without relying on preheating:** XF-102G
- **Light-green process differentiation:** XF-505

### Confirm Before Selection

Peak and continuous temperature; dwell time; reflow or wave-solder route; label position; direct solder exposure; flux and wash chemistry; number of wash cycles; ESD requirements; PCB surface energy; barcode density; ribbon and laser compatibility.

### ETIA Application Support

ETIA helps narrow the material, arrange samples and convert the selected construction into the required format. Production approval should be based on testing through the customer’s complete manufacturing process.

### Use the Actual Process—Not the Highest Possible Temperature

Not every PCB label requires reflow-level heat resistance. A label may be applied after reflow but before cleaning, inspection, conformal coating or later assembly. In these cases, chemical resistance, adhesion, print durability and ESD behavior may matter more than extreme peak temperature.

Selecting a non-reflow construction can avoid unnecessary material cost while still providing reliable identification through the required wash and handling stages.

### Material Directions

- Polyimide for combined chemical resistance and useful heat resistance
- ESD/static-dissipative options for sensitive components and controlled production areas
- Matte or gloss surfaces according to barcode imaging, glare and visual requirements
- Permanent adhesives selected according to PCB surface and cleanliness

### Questions That Define the Material

When is the label applied? Does it encounter solvent, aqueous wash, flux residue or conformal-coating chemistry? What is the maximum temperature after application? Is static dissipation required? Will the label remain on the finished assembly?

### ETIA Application Support

ETIA compares material capability with the real process instead of automatically specifying the most heat-resistant option. Samples should be tested with the intended print, surface, cleaner and exposure sequence.

### Automation Requires More Than a Durable Face Material

A material may survive the PCB process but still fail to dispense consistently. Automatic application depends on the entire converted construction: face stiffness, adhesive, liner release, die-cut geometry, edge quality, web direction, spacing, sensor registration and label presentation.

The material must satisfy both sets of requirements—dispensing performance during application and durability after placement.

### What ETIA Evaluates

- Application point: before reflow, after reflow or post-process
- Required resistance to heat, flux, cleaning chemistry and abrasion
- ESD/static-dissipative requirements
- Label stiffness and minimum dimensions for reliable peel behavior
- Liner, pitch, gap, web direction and roll configuration
- Sensor method, feed accuracy and application tolerance
- Printer, laser marker, feeder or print-and-apply integration

### A Practical Qualification Path

First qualify the material through the PCB process. Then test the converted roll through the actual dispensing equipment at production speed. Approval should cover release consistency, placement accuracy, adhesion and barcode readability after processing.

### Two Different Functions, One Process-Specific Selection

**Masking dots and discs** temporarily cover holes, contacts, components or defined areas during soldering, coating, painting or other process exposure. They must seal the target area, resist the relevant temperature and chemistry, and remove according to the specified residue requirement.

**Inspection arrows and markers** identify defects, rework locations, test points or quality-control findings. They prioritize visibility, controlled adhesion and clean removal during inspection and repair workflows.

### Define the Requirement

- Maximum temperature and dwell time
- Solder, flux, coating, solvent or cleaning exposure
- Surface: solder mask, metal, plastic, glass or another substrate
- Required masking precision and edge seal
- Removal timing and acceptable residue level
- Color and visibility for inspection
- Manual placement or automated dispensing

### Important Selection Note

“Removable” is process-dependent. Adhesion and residue can change after heat, chemicals and dwell time. Test the exact material on the actual surface through the full exposure and removal sequence.

### Durable Identification Created by Laser

Laser-markable labels create characters, barcodes and 2D codes by changing or removing a designed material layer. The result can provide crisp, permanent contrast without ink or ribbon, making the technology attractive for automated, high-throughput traceability.

Laser-markable polyimide constructions are available for demanding PCB processes involving high temperature, reflow, solvents, chemicals and harsh cleaning. Static-dissipative options can be considered for ESD-controlled electronics production.

### The Material and Laser Must Be Qualified Together

- Laser type and wavelength
- Power, speed, frequency and focus
- Required contrast and code density
- Heat-affected zone and dimensional stability
- Fume extraction and process safety
- Adhesive and liner behavior
- Reflow, wash, solvent and abrasion exposure
- ESD requirements and automation format

### Not the Same as Direct Part Marking

A laser-markable label is an adhesive material marked by laser and then applied—or marked after application depending on the system. Direct part marking modifies the component surface itself. Choose the route according to surface risk, process flexibility, replacement needs and traceability design.

### ETIA Application Support

ETIA supports material selection and sampling. Final approval requires marking trials on the intended laser system followed by verification through the full PCB process and barcode grading method.
